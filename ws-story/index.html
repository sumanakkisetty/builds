How a solo founder — with no team, no funding, no desk requirement — built a production enterprise platform from scratch in 6 months. And why it took 20 years to get here.

The Human Story

He was not supposed to build this.

Suman Akkisetty is not who you would expect to build an enterprise software platform.

He grew up in a small town in India — an arts student, not a computer science graduate, not an engineering student. Computers were a subject, not a major. But from the moment he first saw a system respond to code in grade 12, something clicked that never let go. He taught himself. He showed up to the IT industry on passion alone, and built a career spanning two decades at some of the world's largest technology firms.

For twenty years, while working full-time, he carried a vision. Not a vague idea — a specific, recurring dream of building a product that would touch millions of people and solve a real problem he kept running into everywhere he worked. He attempted it before. Multiple times. Each attempt failed. The seed didn't die.

At 40 years old, with two decades of industry experience and the arrival of capable AI, he finally had everything he needed. Not a team. Not funding. Not a MacBook and a standing desk. Just the accumulated weight of twenty years of wanting to build something, a phone, an internet connection, and a clear vision of what the world needed.

"I wasn't trying to start a company. I was trying to solve a problem I kept running into everywhere I worked. And I figured — with AI this capable — maybe I could just build the thing myself." Suman Akkisetty — Founder & Architect
The Setup

The desk was optional.

Most software is built at a desk. Fixed hours, fixed location, fixed machine. Suman's approach was different — not because he abandoned the laptop, but because he refused to be chained to it.

The laptop was always there when needed. But so was the phone — on the couch, mid-walk, on a commute, right before sleep. Wherever a window of time opened up, features got shipped. An AI handled the heavy lifting as a co-developer, making it possible to move fast without a full environment set up in front of you. A lean self-hosted cloud stack, chosen to be fast to iterate on from anywhere.

On a recent trip to Toronto, he shipped SSL certificates for a new domain, wrote full product documentation, and wired up an automated setup wizard — all from a mobile browser, no laptop on the trip. That's not a hack. That's the workflow.

The Inflection Point

18× faster overnight.

The project began on a local machine in November 2025. Standard setup — Go backend, local database, local IDE. Progress was real but slow: 53 commits over 4 months, tethered to a desk.

In March 2026, the entire development workflow moved to the cloud. An AI co-developer ran persistently on a remote server, accessible from any device. The desk stopped being a requirement. The numbers speak for themselves:

The AI Partner

This is not autocomplete.

In a traditional team, a senior engineer owns the full delivery lifecycle — they understand the codebase, write the code, run the tests, catch regressions, deploy, monitor, and document. That role requires years of context about the product.

In this setup, the AI is that senior engineer. It holds the full context of every module, every architectural decision, every past bug, every pending task — across sessions, across weeks, across the entire history of the build. It doesn't just write code when asked. It proactively flags security issues, questions redundant features before building them, maintains structured session logs, and operates the full pipeline from commit to deploy to notification.

The developer's role shifted from doing the work to directing the work — reviewing decisions, setting priorities, approving actions. That is a fundamentally different relationship between a human and a machine than anything that existed before large language models.

"The most remarkable thing is not the technology. It is the stubbornness of the vision." — 20 years in the making
