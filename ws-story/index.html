The Human Story

He spent 20 years watching the problem.
Then he stopped watching.

Suman Akkisetty grew up in a small town in India. Computers were just another subject on the timetable. But something happened in grade 12 when he first saw a machine respond to code he typed. He couldn't explain it. He just knew he wanted more of that feeling.

He taught himself everything. Got into the IT industry on passion, and spent the next two decades working at some of the biggest technology firms in the world.

The whole time, there was this idea in the back of his head. Not vague, not just a "someday" thought. A specific problem he kept seeing everywhere he worked. He tried building something about it a few times over the years. Nothing stuck. Life got in the way. But the idea never went away.

With close to two decades of experience and AI finally capable enough to make this possible, he decided to actually do it. No investment, no team, no one to direct, no one to guide. Just a bare metal personal laptop, a phone, a cloud server with 1 GB RAM, 45 GB disk and a 2-core CPU, and the stubborn refusal to let the idea die again.

"I wasn't trying to start a company. I kept running into the same problem everywhere I worked and at some point I just thought, why not build it myself?" Suman Akkisetty,
Builder and Architect
The Setup

The desk was never required.

He didn't set out to build software without a proper workstation. It just turned out that way. The laptop was there when it was there. But a lot of the time it wasn't, and waiting for it to be there wasn't something he was willing to do.

So features got written on the couch, on walks, during commutes, right before sleep. An AI handled the heavy lifting as a coding partner, which meant it was possible to make real progress without a full setup in front of you. A small cloud server, a lean stack, and nothing that tied him to a specific location.

On a trip to Toronto he shipped SSL certificates for a new domain, wrote the product documentation, and wired up an automated install wizard, all from a mobile browser with no laptop in the bag. That's just how the workflow ended up working.

The Inflection Point

18x faster. Overnight.

The first four months were slow. Local machine, standard setup, 53 commits across November through February. Real progress, but grinding.

Then in March 2026 a thought crossed his mind that seemed almost too simple to take seriously. What if the entire development environment, the compiler, the AI, the server, the logs, all of it, lived in the cloud, and the only thing he needed locally was a browser tab? He had never heard anyone describe building production software this way. It wasn't in any tutorial. It just seemed like it might work. That instinct turned out to be the most important technical decision of the whole project. The security had to be tight: triple-gated admin access, encrypted credentials, rate limiting, org-level data isolation on every query. The kind of setup that normally takes a security team. Built by one person, verified by one AI, running on hardware most developers wouldn't consider seriously.

There was one more constraint that never made it into the commit log. Every AI session has a token limit, and he was hitting it constantly. He learned to front-load the important decisions, compress context without losing it, and work smarter inside those limits. He also knew this couldn't go on indefinitely. Real commitments, real life. The clock was always running. That pressure is in every commit from March onwards.

The AI Partner

Not autocomplete. A real collaborator.

People hear "built with AI" and picture someone typing prompts and copying answers. That's not what happened here. The AI held full context of the entire codebase across every session: decisions made three weeks ago, architectural tradeoffs, security gaps, things that didn't make sense. It flagged problems before they landed in production and pushed back when something was wrong. Suman's job became deciding what to build and in what order. That's a genuinely different way of working than anything that existed a few years ago.

"Twenty years of trying to find the right moment. Turns out the moment was always going to be when the tools finally caught up." Suman Akkisetty

The Hours

This was built at night.

Suman held a full-time job throughout the entire build. Every commit in this repository was pushed in the evenings, through the night, and across weekends — while the working day belonged to someone else. There was no sabbatical. No runway. No "building full-time." Just the hours that were left over, used completely.

3am was the most productive hour of the build. 196 commits were pushed between 3am and 4am — the time when the house was quiet, the phone was silent, and the only thing left to do was write code. On 67 separate nights, the last commit was logged somewhere between 1am and 4am.

March 17 to April 20, 2026: 35 days without missing a single day of commits. From March onwards, not a single weekend was skipped — every Saturday and Sunday through to launch had code shipped. A full-time job during the day. A platform being built at night. The streak was not planned — it was just what happened when the problem mattered enough.
