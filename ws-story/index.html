The Human Story

Nobody saw this coming. Including him.

Suman Akkisetty grew up in a small town in India. Computers were just another subject on the timetable. But something happened in grade 12 when he first saw a machine respond to code he typed. He couldn't explain it. He just knew he wanted more of that feeling.

He taught himself everything. Got into the IT industry on passion, and spent the next two decades working at some of the biggest technology firms in the world.

The whole time, there was this idea in the back of his head. Not vague, not just a "someday" thought. A specific problem he kept seeing everywhere he worked. He tried building something about it a few times over the years. Nothing stuck. Life got in the way. But the idea never went away.

With close to two decades of experience and AI finally capable enough to make this possible, he decided to actually do it. No investment, no team, no one to direct, no one to guide. Just a bare metal personal laptop, a phone, a cloud server with 1 GB RAM, 45 GB disk and a 2-core CPU, and the stubborn refusal to let the idea die again.

"I wasn't trying to start a company. I kept running into the same problem everywhere I worked and at some point I just thought, why not build it myself?" Suman Akkisetty, Builder and Architect
The Setup

The desk was never required.

He didn't set out to build software without a proper workstation. It just turned out that way. The laptop was there when it was there. But a lot of the time it wasn't, and waiting for it to be there wasn't something he was willing to do.

So features got written on the couch, on walks, during commutes, right before sleep. An AI handled the heavy lifting as a coding partner, which meant it was possible to make real progress without a full setup in front of you. A small cloud server, a lean stack, and nothing that tied him to a specific location.

On a trip to Toronto he shipped SSL certificates for a new domain, wrote the product documentation, and wired up an automated install wizard, all from a mobile browser with no laptop in the bag. That's just how the workflow ended up working.

The Inflection Point

18x faster. Overnight.

The first four months were slow. Local machine, standard setup, 53 commits across November through February. Real progress, but grinding.

Then in March 2026 something changed. He moved the entire development environment to a cloud VM and set the AI coding partner to run there persistently, always on, always in context, accessible from any browser anywhere in the world. No IDE to install. No local environment to configure. No machine to be tied to. The whole stack lived in the cloud: code, builds, deployments, logs. The only thing on his end was a browser window. To an outside observer it would have looked like someone doing email. What was actually happening was production software being shipped in real time.

It was also running on a 1 GB server. Not because of some philosophical commitment to constraints, but because that's what was available for free. The security had to be tight: triple-gated admin access, encrypted credentials, rate limiting, org-level data isolation on every query. The kind of setup that normally takes a security team. Built by one person, verified by one AI, running on hardware most developers wouldn't consider seriously.

There was another constraint that never made it into the commit log. Tokens. Every AI session has a limit, and he was hitting it constantly. He learned to work smarter inside those limits, to front-load the important decisions, to compress context without losing it. He also knew this couldn't go on indefinitely. Real commitments, real life. The clock was always running. That pressure is in every commit from March onwards.

The AI Partner

Not autocomplete. A real collaborator.

People hear "built with AI" and picture someone typing prompts and copying answers. That's not what happened here.

The AI held full context of the entire codebase across every session. It knew the decisions made three weeks ago and why. It flagged security gaps before they became problems, pushed back on features that didn't make sense, and ran the whole pipeline from writing code through to deployment and notification. It didn't need to be told the same things twice.

Suman's job became deciding what to build and in what order, reviewing the work, and making the calls. That's a genuinely different way of working than anything that existed a few years ago.

"Twenty years of trying to find the right moment. Turns out the moment was always going to be when the tools finally caught up." Suman Akkisetty
